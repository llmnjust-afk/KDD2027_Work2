\begin{abstract}
Large language model (LLM) agents are increasingly deployed on data-science tasks, yet existing benchmarks report only aggregate success rates, masking \emph{where} and \emph{why} agents fail. In this work we introduce \textbf{AgentFail}, a diagnostic framework that decomposes every agent failure into four stages---Planning, Tool-Use, Execution, and Interpretation---and distinguishes \emph{silent failures} (code executes without error but yields a wrong answer) from \emph{loud failures} (code crashes). We further propose \emph{counterfactual causal replay}, which re-executes a failed trace with the originating step replaced by the ground-truth action to attribute the root cause. We evaluate five frontier LLMs across 4875 runs on three task suites: 80 controlled trap tasks, 23 real UCI datasets, and 200 real DSBench financial-analysis tasks. Our findings reveal a striking \emph{failure bifurcation}: on solvable tasks, strong models (GPT-4o, DeepSeek-V3) exhibit a 100\% silent-failure rate---every failure is a wrong answer rather than a crash---while weak models fail loudly with execution errors; on very hard tasks, all models fail loudly. Critically, agents never self-correct: recovery rate is 0\% across all five models and 4875 runs. A failure-aware verifier helps only the weakest model (Qwen3-max, +24.9\%, $p<0.001$) but harms stronger models, indicating that silent failures resist simple automatic correction. Our taxonomy achieves 79--88\% causal-attribution success and our diagnostic metrics---silent-failure rate, propagation depth, and token-per-success---expose structural failure patterns that aggregate accuracy conceals. We release all code, data, and 4875 annotated traces to facilitate reproducible failure diagnosis.
\end{abstract}
