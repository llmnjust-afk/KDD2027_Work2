Large language model (LLM) agents increasingly solve data-science tasks by writing and executing code, yet aggregate success rates conceal where and how they fail. We introduce \textbf{AgentFail}, a diagnostic framework with five failure stages---Analytical-Plan, Code-Generation, Runtime, Output-Mismatch, and Answer-Error---and an independent observability label that distinguishes silent from explicitly visible failures. We evaluate five LLMs over 5{,}984 runs on 318 tasks from synthetic, UCI, and DSBench suites. After separating 2{,}218 infrastructure failures, we analyse 3{,}602 valid runs and release a 150-trace human-adjudicated gold set. Its observed stages are Output-Mismatch (77), Runtime (39), Answer-Error (22), and Code-Generation (12); Analytical-Plan has no final cases. On the newly annotated 103-trace tranche, raw pre-adjudication agreement is Fleiss' $\kappa=.350$ for stage and $.590$ for observability, exposing a difficult Output-Mismatch/Analytical-Plan boundary that we resolve by consensus adjudication. Our revised oracle-assisted rule classifier obtains 91.3\% accuracy (95\% bootstrap CI [86.7, 95.3]) and 90.3\% macro-F1 over the four observed classes; observability accuracy is 99.3\%. Silent-failure rates remain task-dependent (86\% synthetic, 4\% UCI, 19\% DSBench). A six-condition recovery benchmark yields 15.7\% recovery overall (10.4\% silent versus 36.7\% loud), while oracle repair matches its no-op control (64\%). We release code, traces, adjudications, splits, and reproducible baselines.
